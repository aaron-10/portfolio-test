3D printed rotary subwoofer

2021 - Present

Overview

A rotary subwoofer is a fascinating type of subwoofer: it is a fan that can change the pitch of the blades very quickly. By switching the direction of airflow back and forth very quickly it can generate sound waves. I've always wanted to build one just to hear what such deep bass sounds like, so I built one while taking a course on Acoustics. For this course I also worked on simulations in COMSOL, but it turns out to be quite tricky to simulate.



Background

A traditional speaker setup consists of speakers for the higher frequency range and a subwoofer for the lower frequency range. The higher frequency range is covered quite well by the midrange speakers and tweeters. However, the lower frequency range is not fully covered by a traditional subwoofer. The response of most subwoofers already rolls off above 20Hz, and most subwoofers certainly do not cover the infrasonic range (f < 20Hz).


A rotary subwoofer can solve this gap in frequency response. It can generate very low frequencies and can even go down to 0Hz, which is no useful sound but is just constant displacement of air. Whereas the limitation of traditional conus-based subwoofers is a lower frequency limit, the limiting factor for a rotary subwoofer is actually an upper frequency limit.


Design

The modulation mechanism consists of two discs that can move with respect to each other. The blades are connected to one of the discs via some tiny bearings. The other disc is connected to the blades via small rods that push and pull the blades. The voice coil moves this second disc, and thus changes the pitch of the blades.

Building

I used an old fan motor to make the subwoofer rotate, and to module the blade pitch I used the voice coil of an old subwoofer. All the black parts are 3D printed and the blades were laser cut from some thin plywood.

Testing

The subwoofer can be placed in the opening of a window with plywood around it to make it as airtight as possible. This turns the entire house into the enclosure.

In the end it worked pretty well. It can even play some music. It got up to a maximum frequency of around 32Hz, but it really struggles at such high frequencies.


The sound of the really deep bass is quite unsettling. The frequencies lower than 20Hz are not really audible of course, but you still 'sense' the sound for frequencies slightly lower than 20Hz. Much lower than 20Hz is not detectable. The microphone of my phone could pic it up down to around 12Hz, below which it also stopped detecting the sound.
